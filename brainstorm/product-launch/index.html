Enduring What?

Before we built anything,
we asked what the sea takes from him.

A man on a fast boat, hours at sea, in the heat, in the storm. What does that actually do to his body? We listed it honestly. This list is where every product began.

The pounding

Every wave slams the boat, and the slam goes into his back. He cannot feel the damage adding up — but after years, his spine can end his career.

So, Our Assistance and Honouring

We assist him with machines that carry his burden.
We honour him by paying attention to his body.

To assist means to take some of the sea's weight off him. The seat takes the pounding so his spine does not. The gear watches his temperature and his heart so he does not have to guess how much he has left.

To honour means his condition is never invisible again. His commander sees him. If he falls, the boat knows in seconds. If he is wounded, the boat knows where. A man who is watched over is a man who is valued.

And we made one rule for ourselves, so this never becomes surveillance:

We only measure what changes what we do for him.

Every sensor in this room exists because it leads to an action — a warning, an adjustment, a rescue. If a measurement helps no one, we do not take it.

First Answer · The Seat

The sea pounds his spine.
The seat takes it instead.

Wolver One is a suspension seat — like a good motorcycle absorbs the road, the seat absorbs the wave. But a suspension only protects well when it is set for the man sitting on it. A heavy man and a light man need different settings. So we taught the seat to fit itself.

The seat carries a small tank of gas — the same idea as a submarine's air tanks. Each adjustment uses a little of it. When the tank runs low, the seat does not stop working — it simply holds one good standard setting until our next service visit, when we change the tank.

It can run out of adjustments. It can never become a broken seat.

One more thing, for safety: the seat will never adjust itself while someone is sitting on it. It checks that it is empty first, every time, by machine — not by promise.

And while he rides, the seat counts every slam it absorbs. Over months, that count tells his commander how much pounding each man has taken — the damage that today, nobody can see.

Second Answer · The Pelt

Second Answer · The Pelt

The heat, the cold, the exhaustion —
now the boat can see them.

The Pelt is sports gear — like the base-layer shirts athletes wear. Light, breathable, quick to dry. The operator puts it on for comfort. Inside it, small sensor pods quietly read his body: his heartbeat, his temperature at several points, his movement, whether he is in the water.

Why many points and not one? Because one reading can lie. A single thermometer says "he is fine" while one part of his body is already in trouble. Many points tell the truth about where he is winning and where he is losing.

And that same idea opens a door for later: if a man is wounded, the point nearest the wound changes first. One day, the boat will not just know someone is hurt — it will know where.

The operator chooses how much he wears:

Light

Bands only

Arm and leg bands. Fastest to put on, easiest to wash. Reads the basics.

Standard

Vest + bands

Adds the chest — the heartbeat and the torso. This is what stands here today.

Full

Full suit

The whole body covered. Reads the most, for the hardest missions.

Wear more, and the boat sees more. Wear less, and washing is easier. His choice.

Third Answer · The Dashboard

The commander no longer guesses.
He sees his men.

One screen at the helm. On it: each man's heartbeat, his temperature, his seat moving with the waves, his body posture — live. Not a table of numbers. A picture of his crew.

If a man goes into the water, the boat knows in seconds — not because one signal guessed it, but because four separate signs agree: his seat is empty, his gear lost contact with the boat, his body fell, and his gear touched water. Four signs together do not cry wolf.

Two promises are built into the machine itself, not written on paper:

First — the system never controls the boat and never overrides the man. It watches, it warns, it advises. The operator always commands. The life-critical warnings run on simple, fixed electronics — the kind that do the same thing every time, like a smoke detector.

Second — his body's data never leaves the vessel. It is stored on board, like an aircraft's black box. What the fleet learns from it travels; the man's private data does not. The institution that owns the vessel decides everything about its use.

And Then, We Keep Coming Back

We do not sell and disappear.
We serve, on a schedule.

Every month, Wolverpelagén returns to the vessel. We change the seat's gas tank. We inspect and service the seat and the gear. We deliver a report of what the missions showed — how much pounding each seat absorbed, how the crew's condition held up.

This is how the products stay excellent for years in saltwater, not months. It is also how we keep learning: every visit, every vessel, teaches the next generation of the product.

A seat is sold once. Care is given for years. The care is the real product.
